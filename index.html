    The U.S. Surgeon General states, “It is simply in the best interest for a woman to not drink alcohol.”

    This health assessment will estimate how your pregnancy lifestyle decisions and personal history impact your child’s risk of having a fetal alcohol spectrum disorder (FASD).

     Note

    This risk assessment is only intended for women who have consumed any amount of alcohol at some point during their pregnancy. If you have abstained from alcohol while trying to conceive and during your pregnancy, your child is not at risk for a FASD.

    Risk Description

    Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD) is an umbrella term describing the range of effects that can occur in an individual prenatally exposed to alcohol.

    Making Healthy Decisions

    • Abstain from alcohol for the remainder of your pregnancy.
    • Talk to your physician, other healthcare professional, or contact Alcoholics Anonymous if you need help to stop drinking drinking or using other substances.
    • Avoid tobacco, marijuana, illegal and legal drugs that can harm your baby’s development.
    • Find a stable support group or community.
    • Eat a healthy, balanced diet and talk to your physician about taking prenatal vitamins.
    • If you are concerned that your child may have a FASD, speak to a physician about receiving a proper diagnosis.
    • Visit the NOFAS website for more information on FASD and to connect to a resource directory

    Important Note: While there are some known risk factors for a child to develop FASD, there is no guarantee that one factor will lead to an alcohol-related disorder. There is still more research to be done in regards to how genetics affect an individual's risk for FASD. Similarly, everyone's metabolism functions at different rates, so risk may vary among individuals. The only way to completely prevent your child from developing a fetal alcohol spectrum disorder (FASD) is to abstain from drinking while pregnant.

    Medical Disclaimer:

    The content in this health assessment is based off scientific evidence. This health risk calculator is not intended to diagnose or guarantee that an individual will have a certain condition. Please see a physician or qualified health care provider for a complete health assessment and diagnosis.

    Accuracy of Scoring:

    While there are known factors that increase an individual’s risk for alcohol-related birth defects, there is still more research to be done. Beyond lifestyle factors, it is suggested that genetics can impact the risk for a child to be born with FASD, but no genome wide association has been found. Similarly, alcohol metabolism varies between individuals and pregnancy affects that metabolism in a variety of ways. The only way to completely prevent an alcohol-related birth defect is for a woman to abstain from drinking during her pregnancy.

    Anonymity of Data:

    Pregnancy & Alcohol

    The National Institute on Alcohol Abuse and Alcoholism (NIAAA) and the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) define binge drinking as 4 or more drinks on one occasion for women. According to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS), moderate drinking is up to 1 drink per day for women.

    The U.S. Surgeon General advices women to not drink while they are pregnant.
